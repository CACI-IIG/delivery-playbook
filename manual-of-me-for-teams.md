### Overview

Manual of Me is a discussion canvas to encourage self reflection, and sharing of how we as people operate, and how best we work with others. The manual is made up of a range of questions (some default, some customised) for individuals to fill out, and then share, such as “I prefer to communicate in these ways…”, or “I am at my best when…”.   
  
This tool can be used to:
- Support individuals with their wellbeing and mental health 
- As the basis of a team building session, first through individual, then creating a shared manual (detailed below)
- As an ongoing resource for an individual or team.

### Team Building Session 

**Aim**: Increase collective understanding of how a group can work together
**Who**: All Team members
**Time**: 1:30 - 3hrs hours

**Broad Facilitation Guide / Agenda**
- **Preparation before the session**
    Create a manual of me template to suit the team and its growth areas. Give everyone a way to fill them out and share them (paper works best, but can do via mural)

- **Set the Scene - 2 mins**
    Introduce the session aims (don't mention manuals yet). Link to teams feedback to reinforce why the session is happening.

- **Collaboratively build a skill list - <15 mins** (Skill Customisation). 
    Ask the group what skills they think are important to their roles / team, and get them to agree a list of the most important ones (~5 total). Can do this with post-its, open discussion. Group together similar ones to get a broad spread of elements important to the teams overall success.  Can be specific (testing / time management) or broad (management / leadership). If there are role descriptions it may be worth alluding to them.

- **Gather additional team questions - <10 mins**
    Ask the group “what questions do you want to know about each other?”. Give some examples of questions you already have planned on the manual, but check in if the group wants to know anything extra. 

- **Introduce Manuals of Me - 3 Mins**
    - Outline the approach for the rest of the session (individual manuals, sharing, collective manual)
    - Reiterate The goal isn't to make a manual, the goal is to build collective understanding.
    - Facilitator show own manual to lead by example with the questions and answers.

- **Instructions for Skills (Skill Customisation)- 2 mins**
    - Get everyone to add the skills they have listed to the manual, and instruct that they can fill in X boxes total on the chart to indicate their relative strengths and weaknesses.
    - X = (Total skills * 2) + (total skills / 2). This will give everyone (to few) points to add to the chart, and force everyone to highlight relative difference between their skills. See Example below, This does not work as well if you dont limit the number 

- **Individual Manual creation ~10 mins**
    - Quite time provided for all individuals to have a go at filling in their own manual.
    - Allow questions, but broadly ensure everyone has focus time to write an answer in all sections
    - Once the group seems ready, move on

- **Break out discussions ~1hr (made of 10-15min rotations)**
    - Depending on team size, break group into pairs (or triples if large).
    - Ask pairs to discuss. 
        - Where they are the same / align
        - Where they differ
    - You can ask pairs to write down these points on a whiteboard to help them see the key points, but this is only to aid conversation, 
    - Let discussions run for ~15 mins. This is prime coaching time to support pairs to tackle hard conversations, and observe when to move on.
    - Rotate 1 person per pair onto the next group. Repeat until at least a majority of individuals have spoken 1-2-1. Encourage them to self organise if they don't know who to rotate to next. Might be they want to form slightly larger groups.

- **Team reflections 30+ mins**
    - Bring everyone back together to collaborate on team wide points. Encourage open discussion to fill in a team wide canvas representing the important elements of how the team should be worked with. Draw out areas where there wasn't agreement and encourage the group to tackle it. Remember, the manual is less important than the discussion / breaking through team challenges. 
    - **Alternative:** Rather than a team manual, move to a more standard retrospective approach of working through key points individuals have noticed through discussions. Encourage solving disagreements in the room, then capturing tangible agreements / actions. 

- **Wrap up - 5 mins**
    - Confirm any tangible outcomes, actions and agreements
    - Confirm what the team want to do with their manuals next (publish for team & re-use, keep as individuals, bin)

### General Notes

Having run this with Engineering Teams and Leadership teams, I have found a need to be very dynamic with timings, actively reading the room to see what it needs. You may find that the group quickly move away from the manuals in the discussion phase, this is fine. They are just there to illicit hard conversations. 

If you are the facilitator, its critical you let the group lead as much as possible. Absolutely nudge with various prepared questions and skills to target the things the team needs to improve on, but as a team building technique ensuring you let them set their own skills and extra questions helps them feel ownership over what they are doing (rather than being tested)

### Customisation: Skills

This is an element you can use with teams on its own, but compliments the Manual of Me really well.   
Skill points are a means to generate strong self reflection of strengths and weaknesses in a safe way, and gives a way for teams to discuss how they support each other & cross skill. 

Much like building a new video game character, you assign a limited number of points to a set of skills. Rather than Strength, Wisdom or Constitution, you or your team can pick ones key to your work / roles like (Communication, AWS, leadership, testing, ect ect).   
  
The idea of having a limited number of points to assign helps individuals have a hard think about their strengths and weaknesses (without having to go into nitty gritty). Do they assign points in everything and appear 'broad', or highlight key strengths they bring to the team. 

As this is done in relative terms for the individual (not against each other), it avoids conflict of comparison or disagreement of who is ‘best’ at a skill. 

Once completed by individuals, it can be used in a team discussion to highlight how we are all different, may have gaps as a wider team, and move to how we can support each other to build skills across the team
